Analog IC Design

Course Outline

Analog circuits are becoming more important today because they provide the interface between the real world signals and digital processing systems. At the lowest level of design, the designer must understand the MOS transistor characteristics and how these can be used to build analog circuits. This course provides the trainees with background on MOS transistor physics, its operating characteristics, and how it can be used to implement basic analog building blocks such as bias circuits, and differential amplifiers. This will enable the trainees to design and implement more complex circuits.

The following topics are included in this 1-week course:

  • MOS transistor biasing and small-signal model
  • Single stage amplifiers
  • Active loads, current sources and mirrors
  • MOS capacitances
  • Differential amplifiers
  • Frequency response of single and differential amplifiers

Learning Outcomes

After taking this course, you should be able to:

  • Describe the conditions to bias a MOS transistor in a desired region
  • Describe how the small-signal parameters vary with the operating region
  • Derive and simulate the equivalent two-port network parameters of single stage amplifiers
  • Obtain the size of a transistor active load to achieve required amplifier performance
  • Describe how the frequency response is affected by the amplifier parameters
  

Operational Amplifier Design

Course Outline

A major component in many of today’s analog electronic circuits is the operational amplifier. In practical applications, advanced amplifier topologies are used to achieve high gain, high input impedance, and low output impedance. This course provides the trainees with adequate background and experience in the analysis and design of a two-stage CMOS amplifier. It also discusses issues involved in designing this amplifier for practical applications like the negative feedback.

The following topics are included in this 1-week course:

  • Multi-stage amplifiers
  • Miller Two-Stage OTA DC analysis, small signal analysis, frequency response
  • Feedback circuit analysis, stability analysis
  • Compensation techniques
  • Design trade-offs for a compensated Miller OTA

Learning Outcomes

After taking this course, you should be able to:

  • Describe the key characteristics of the different types of amplifiers
  • Explain the relationship and effects of amplifier specifications with the circuit design parameters
  • Perform stability analysis for a given negative feedback amplifier circuit
  • Apply compensation techniques given an unstable feedback amplifier circuit
  • Design a two-stage Miller OTA systematically to satisfy given specifications and highlight design trade-offs
 

Recommended Background

Trainees for this course are expected be electronics or computer engineering students and are also expected to be familiar with semiconductor physics and circuit analysis.

Participants with basic experience in IC design and those who want to pursue a career in design engineering are also welcome.
